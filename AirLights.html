Airlights
Turn Signal Armband

Project Overview

Purpose: address the safety concerns faced by bicyclists while signaling, particularly in low-light conditions.

Objective: develop a device that helps bicyclists better signal their turns and stay under a $500 budget.

AirLights is an armband device that enhances bicyclist safety by providing intuitive and hands-free turn signals. By nudging the elbow away from the body, a left turn signal is triggered, while nudging it towards the body activates a right turn signal. The armband stops signaling when the elbow is at rest.

Problem Statement

AirLights was inspired by a personal experience of riding my bike home from school. I encountered a dimly lit street and found myself being approached by a car while making a turn. This experience made me realize the risks posed by the absence of signaling lights, especially when sharing the road with passing vehicles.

Considering the multitasking and limited protection provided by a bicycle, I created AirLights to address the challenges of controlling the bike with one hand, signaling with the other arm, and remaining aware of approaching vehicles.

Design Process

Initial Ideas:
A wearable jacket with turn signals: accessories such as a backpack could cover up the signaling mechanism.

Turn signal helmet / tail lights on the bike: would lack seamlessness because the user would have to manually press buttons.

Selected Idea: For intuitiveness and seamlessness, without the need for the user to press any buttons, I opted for an armband.

It is visible with any attire and accessories, users don't need to extend their arm for signaling, which can be dangerous in low-light environments, and is cost-effective.

Prototype 1 & 2

Main component: the accelerometer and gyroscope track the orientation and movement of the user's arm, triggering the appropriate turn signals.

Both prototypes use the same components. However, the first one is made out of an Arduino Uno and a larger breadboard, whereas the second one was made out of an Arduino Nano and a smaller breadboard for compactness.

Prototype 1 costs around $42 dollars and prototype 2 costs around $41.

Additional Documentation & Developments

Potential Developments: Making the device weatherproof.

Calling 911: if heart rate is not detected for a certain amount of time or if the user does not respond to AirLights after falling, 911 is automatically contacted.

More compact and seamless design, with its components embedded into the armband.

Decreasing overall costs.

Switching to other forms of rechargeable power to reduce battery wastage and pollution.
